## 4. Sub-Regime Axes: Regime-Specific Detectors Are Correct; Universal Axis Is a Trap

Your intuition that each regime needs its own sub-regime axes is **correct and should be preserved**. Choppy uses vol × breadth because chop is defined by directionless oscillation—volatility determines whether it's violent or dull, breadth determines whether it's rotational or flat. Bear uses vol × 60d_return because Bear is defined by decline—volatility determines panic vs grind, return determines oversold bounce potential vs capitulation risk. These axes are mechanically appropriate to what each regime *is*, not arbitrary choices.

The temptation to find a "universal axis" (e.g., always use vol × breadth) is a **dimensionality reduction trap** that will destroy regime-specific edge. Bull regime using trend_persistence × leadership_concentration makes perfect sense: Bull is defined by uptrend, so persistence measures trend health and leadership measures whether it's broadening (sustainable) or narrowing (exhaustion). If you forced Bull onto the Bear's vol × 60d_return axes, you'd miss the critical leadership-rotation dynamics that differentiate healthy Bull (FAANG + midcaps participating) from fragile Bull (only 5 stocks holding indices up). The regime defines the relevant questions; the sub-regime axes answer those questions.

However, there **is** a meta-pattern worth codifying: each regime's primary axis is always the defining characteristic (Choppy=directionlessness via breadth, Bear=decline via return, Bull=trend via persistence), and the secondary axis is always a risk/volatility measure that splits the primary axis into manageable flavors. This meta-structure can guide new regime discoveries (e.g., Distributional regime might use vol × put/call_ratio to split topping behavior into orderly vs panic). Document this as an architectural principle: **"Primary axis = regime definition, secondary axis = risk flavor"** rather than trying to force universal feature sets.

The tri-modal structure (hot 15% / warm / cold 85%) appearing in both Choppy and Bear suggests that **extremes matter** across regimes—most of the time, conditions are "normal" (cold), but the tails (hot) exhibit qualitatively different behavior that demands separate treatment. This is a universal insight: always look for regime-extreme detectors (top 10-20% of observations on relevant axes) as sub-regime candidates. But the axes themselves should remain regime-specific. Your approach is architecturally sound; resist the urge to unify prematurely.

This prevents emotional spiraling while maintaining systematic discipline.
